Many companies focus almost exclusively on code, rapid release and feature implementation, letting user experience (UX) and user interface (UI) slip into the background, with the result of products that have chaotic navigability, graphic inconsistencies and a frustrating user experience. A good product is not just the result of well-written code, but of well-thought-out design, and to achieve effective design you need a serious, structured and professional design system.

Many entrepreneurs and managers see UX and UI as a secondary detail: the logic is often that the important thing is that it works, and the interface can be fixed later. This mentality, however, leads to products that are difficult to use, requiring constant explanations or training, repelling users instead of attracting them. The reasons for this approach are varied and intertwined. Those who make decisions often do not know the difference between UX and UI, seeing them as simple embellishments of the product rather than genuine levers of quality. Progress tends to be measured in lines of code written, without evaluating the quality of the user experience, and many companies have no dedicated designers or, if they do, do not give them the right decision-making space. On top of all this comes pressure to go live: the urgency to release the product as soon as possible leads to sacrificing everything that is not strictly necessary for operation, resulting in messy products that lack visual coherence and consistent interaction.

##### What is a design system?

A design system is a set of rules, guidelines, components and best practices that guarantee visual and functional consistency to a digital product. It is not just a set of colors and fonts, but a genuine design architecture that spans several distinct levels. A consistent visual language covers colors, typography, icons, spacing and visual hierarchies that make the product recognizable and readable. Reusable components, such as buttons, forms and tables, standardize all interface elements and eliminate redundancy. Interaction patterns define how elements behave, including transitions, animations and error and success states. Finally, accessibility ensures that the product is usable by people with disabilities, following WCAG guidelines: ignoring them means excluding a significant portion of users, a problem that many companies underestimate until they face the consequences. A good design system not only improves the quality of the final product, but reduces development times, avoids resource waste and simplifies the work of designers and developers, who can focus on innovation rather than reinventing the same elements every time.

##### UX and UI: investments, not costs

A well-designed product not only improves user satisfaction, but also reduces support and assistance costs. When an interface is clear, well-organized and intuitive, users do not need continuous clarifications, and a curated UX means fewer errors, fewer abandonments and greater loyalty. User tolerance for frustrating experiences has dropped significantly in recent years, and in a digital market where alternatives are always a click away, a poorly designed interface is not merely an aesthetic inconvenience but a direct cost on conversion and retention rates. A user who finds an app or platform complicated to use will not invest time trying to adapt, but will look elsewhere, taking their network of potential referrals with them.

The reality is that correcting a bad UX after the fact is much more expensive than designing it correctly from the beginning: usability flaws spread through the code, user habits and internal processes, and resolving them requires far more time and resources than a solid design approach from the outset would have demanded.
