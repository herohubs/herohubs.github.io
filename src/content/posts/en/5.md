When we think about operating systems, we often recall the familiar Windows logo or, for some, the iconic minimalism of macOS. But behind the scenes of our digital world there is a less known protagonist, omnipresent and often invisible: Linux. We could define it as the "Swiss army knife" of computing, but that would be reductive, because Linux is not just versatile — it is the foundation on which much of the world's digital infrastructure rests.

Most personal computers still run Windows or macOS, but outside the world of personal computing Linux's presence is practically total. Home routers and internet provider systems run on Linux, as do almost all successful and scalable web servers: about 95% of the 500 most powerful supercomputers in the world use this operating system. Cloud infrastructure, streaming services, social networks, and online gaming platforms are all powered by Linux. Smart devices, from connected appliances to IoT sensors, rely on it for its lightness and reliability. Mobility has also embraced Linux: Tesla and many other manufacturers use customized versions for their vehicles' onboard systems, and the kernel is the basis of Android, which powers the majority of smartphones in the world.

##### The ecosystem of collaboration

One of the reasons Linux is so widespread is its open source nature. This means that anyone can access the source code, modify it, and adapt it to their needs. It is an extraordinary example of global collaboration: thousands of developers around the world contribute to making it better every day.

This collaborative approach has led to the creation of Linux distributions optimized for every need: from enterprise servers like Ubuntu Server or CentOS to embedded systems for IoT devices. The reason Linux dominates so broadly is not accidental, but reflects structural qualities that make it difficult to replace. Linux-based systems can run for years without needing restarts, making them ideal for servers and critical infrastructure. Linux is known for being highly secure, thanks to its modular structure and the active community that quickly fixes vulnerabilities. It can be customized to adapt to any type of hardware, from supercomputers to small IoT sensors, and carries no licensing costs, making it the preferred choice for startups, tech companies, and cloud giants.

##### Continuous innovation

The latest versions of the Linux kernel continue to redefine standards of technological innovation. With each update, the system becomes increasingly versatile, adapting perfectly to the needs of an ever-evolving technological landscape. Thanks to extended support for the latest AMD and Intel processors, Linux establishes itself as an ideal choice for those seeking high-level performance. The optimizations introduced in network configurations and storage make it a solid solution for datacenters and servers handling complex workloads, while on the security front new features are implemented to defend modern infrastructures from increasingly sophisticated attacks.

##### Linux and the future of technology

With the advancement of artificial intelligence, blockchain, and IoT, Linux confirms itself at the center of technological innovation. Projects like Kubernetes, which revolutionize the world of cloud computing, and Android, which dominates the smartphone market, are based on Linux. Also in the educational sector Linux is gaining ground: many universities and technical training courses use it to teach programming, system administration, and cybersecurity, which means that future generations of IT professionals will continue to rely on it.

##### A silent hero

In essence, every time someone turns on a device, watches a streaming video, or books a trip online, they are probably using a system powered by Linux. It is not just an operating system, but the backbone of our digital ecosystem. Its omnipresence is not a coincidence, but the result of decades of development, innovation, and global collaboration. Without Linux, the digital world we know today would not exist.
