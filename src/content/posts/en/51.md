When Sakana AI announced Fugu, the immediate reaction of many commentators was to frame it as yet another model outperforming its predecessors on benchmarks. Headlines multiplied, comparisons with GPT-5 and Claude accumulated, and the by-now familiar pattern of the race toward computational power seemed to be repeating itself once more. The reality is more interesting and, in a sense, more subversive: Fugu is not a model in the traditional meaning of the term. It is a system that coordinates other models, a manager of artificial intelligences rather than an additional artificial intelligence.

##### what fugu actually does

The distinction is not technical but conceptual, and it is worth dwelling on. A classical language model receives an input and produces an output through its own parameters, the billions of weights that encode its capabilities. Fugu works differently: given a request, it breaks it down into components, identifies which model in its pool is best suited to each part, delegates the work, cross-checks the results, and synthesises a single response. In practice it does not perform the work, it organises it. The analogy with the general contractor in construction is apt: a general contractor does not lay bricks or run cables, but ensures that the right people are in the right place at the right time and that the final result is coherent.

The choice of name is not accidental. Fugu is Japanese for pufferfish and continues the aquatic metaphor of Sakana, which means fish in Japanese. The company was founded in Tokyo in 2023 by Llion Jones, co-author of the "Attention Is All You Need" paper that gave rise to transformers, and David Ha, former head of research at Stability AI. The company name itself evokes the collective intelligence of shoals, the idea that a set of coordinated entities can produce more sophisticated behaviour than any individual taken alone.

##### the logic of diminishing returns

There is an implicit hypothesis in this approach that deserves to be made explicit: adding computational power produces diminishing returns. For years progress in artificial intelligence advanced primarily along one dimension, that of scale. Larger models, broader datasets, and more powerful infrastructure produced better systems in a fairly predictable way. That trajectory is showing its limits, not because training ever-larger models is impossible, but because the marginal cost of each further improvement grows while the marginal benefit decreases.

If this is true, the competitive advantage shifts. What matters is no longer only who builds the most powerful brain, but who manages to coordinate available brains most effectively. This is reasoning that the world of human organisation has long understood. The internet did not triumph because it was the most powerful computer, but because it was a network. It did not concentrate power, it distributed and connected it. The result exceeded the capabilities of any centralised machine of the era, not because individual nodes were extraordinary, but because the way they were connected produced something qualitatively different.

##### the parallel with human organisations

The same mechanism appears in companies. Whoever builds a winning organisation rarely does so by finding the single most brilliant employee available on the market. They do it by building systems in which many competent people can coordinate their work effectively, reduce friction activities, specialise where their contribution is most valuable, and integrate with others where specialisation alone is not enough. The overall result of the organisation exceeds the sum of individual contributions not because of the average quality of individuals but because of the quality of coordination among them.

Fugu brings this logic into artificial intelligence explicitly. Instead of competing on parameters and raw-power benchmarks, it poses a different question: given a set of already capable models, what coordination system produces the best results? The answer it offers is a meta-model that has learned not to solve problems but to distribute problems among those best equipped to solve them. It is a subtle but significant distinction, because it shifts the centre of innovation from building the model to building the logic that governs it.

##### what changes in practice

The practical implications of this shift in paradigm are not negligible. An orchestration system like Fugu can in principle improve over time not by training a larger model but by adding new specialised models to its pool or updating existing ones. Improvement does not require starting over; it requires expanding the network. This lowers the barriers for new entrants: there is no need to possess the infrastructure to train a frontier model from scratch, only the logic to coordinate those already available.

Sakana offers Fugu in two variants, one optimised for low latency on everyday tasks and one, Fugu Ultra, intended for complex problems requiring multi-step reasoning on technical, scientific, or legal questions. On reference benchmarks Sakana claims that Fugu Ultra positions itself on par with the most recent frontier models, which, if confirmed in practice, suggests that the capability threshold reachable without training a frontier model of one's own has risen significantly.

##### an open question about the future

The most relevant question remains open: the one concerning the direction in which the field will move in the medium term. If orchestration becomes the primary lever of progress, the future of artificial intelligence could increasingly resemble a team of specialised systems coordinating to tackle what none of them could tackle alone, rather than a solitary genius that can solve any problem it is given. That transition will not happen sharply; monolithic models will continue to improve and orchestration systems will continue to depend on them, but the direction in which Sakana is placing its bet is one in which value lies in the connections more than in the power of the nodes.
