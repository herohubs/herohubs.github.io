Planning and executing software projects is a challenge that never stops testing even the most experienced teams. Every project carries the risk of sliding into recurring problems: sometimes you build something that does not meet users' real needs, other times you exceed timelines and budgets significantly, and often you discover only when work is already well advanced that some fundamental assumptions were wrong. Yet addressing these difficulties in a structured way can make a concrete difference between a project that reaches its goals and one that loses its way. Over the years, an approach has taken shape that rests on three pillars: correctly defining the scope, establishing meaningful milestones, and proceeding with execution that is disciplined but flexible.

##### Project scope

Everything starts from an idea. Someone in the company proposes a new feature, or a need emerges to create an internal tool that automates an existing process. Often, behind these ideas lies an expectation of timelines that is never made explicit: "it will take three months" is a thought that might seem harmless, but it immediately translates into concrete resources such as salaries, team time, and allocated budget. When these expectations stay implicit, the risk is that they surface only once the project has already taken a direction that is difficult to correct.

Defining a clear scope before writing a single line of code is crucial for avoiding misunderstandings that turn out to be costly. This means explicitly aligning the team on objectives, constraints, and priorities: how much time is available for this project? What is the maximum acceptable budget? Which features are essential and which are desirable but not strictly necessary? The more precisely the boundaries are defined, the easier it is to find creative solutions within them, and the simpler it becomes to communicate realistic expectations to all the stakeholders involved. In larger projects, where uncertainty is structurally high, it becomes fundamental to divide the work into phases that can each be evaluated independently, so as not to end up investing an entire year in something that a leaner approach could have validated or discarded in a matter of weeks.

##### Clear milestones

When tackling complex projects, milestones are not simply progress markers but active tools for creating clarity under conditions of uncertainty. A good milestone does not just serve to measure progress toward the final goal; it also tests critical hypotheses, reduces areas of risk, and keeps the team in a position to change direction if something unexpected surfaces. This property, often overlooked during planning, is what distinguishes an effective plan from a sequence of formal deliveries that produces no real knowledge.

Consider the case of a team that needs to develop an internal tool to optimize a complex business process. Instead of immediately building a feature-rich interface, a well-designed first milestone might be the creation of a simple prototype using a low-code tool that allows collecting real feedback from end users without committing months of development effort. If that prototype reveals that the process to be optimized is actually simpler than expected, or that users have different needs from those assumed, that knowledge has enormous value and arrives at a moment when it is still possible to act on it. A well-designed milestone should always be able to answer one question: could we stop here and still have produced something useful? If the answer is yes, the milestone is meaningful; if not, it is just an administrative checkpoint.

##### Execution as a key element

Defining a scope and planning sensible milestones is necessary but not sufficient. What ultimately determines a project's success is how the plan is put into practice day by day, and this operational dimension is often where the best plans come unstuck. During execution, working in short iterations makes it possible to obtain constant feedback from the real world, to correct course quickly when something unexpected emerges, and to keep the team oriented toward tangible results rather than toward the production of work in progress that remains invisible until the very end.

An effective approach to execution involves an initial exploration phase in which several possible solutions to the problem are considered, followed by a progressive convergence toward the most promising one. This dynamic, often described as divergence and convergence, is particularly valuable in innovative projects where it is not clear at the outset which path is the right one. Keeping the field open at the beginning, when the cost of changing direction is low, and narrowing the focus as knowledge accumulates, is a strategy that balances creativity and pragmatism in an effective way. It is equally important to monitor the context in which the team is operating continuously: which other responsibilities are absorbing attention, which impediments are slowing the work down, and whether the assumptions underpinning the plan still hold in light of what has been learned.

##### The ingredients for success

A well-executed software project is never the result of chance, nor of a perfect plan drawn up at the outset. It is the product of a clear vision of objectives, planning flexible enough to absorb the inevitable uncertainty, and disciplined execution that does not abandon adaptation when reality demands it. The organizations that manage their projects most successfully are not those that predict the future, but those that build their processes in ways that allow them to learn quickly and correct direction before mistakes become too costly to absorb. Asking yourself at every stage of the work whether you are solving the right problem with the right resources in the most direct way possible is perhaps the most valuable habit a development team can cultivate.
