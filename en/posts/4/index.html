AI and the revolution in software development

    • AI
      • |
  • January 2025 |
  • 04 Mins read

In recent years, artificial intelligence has stopped being an experimental tool confined to research laboratories and become an integral part of the daily work of millions of developers. This is not a gradual or predictable change but a transformation that is altering in depth the very meaning of writing software, redefining what is expected of a programmer, how the craft is learned, and which skills become central compared to those that are losing relevance.

The daily work changes shape

AI-based tools have changed the way a developer approaches the working day in very concrete terms. Automatic code generation from a natural language description, real-time suggestions while writing, contextual analysis to identify bugs that are not immediately visible, refactoring guided by recurring patterns: all of these capabilities existed in rudimentary form for years, but the quality reached by current models has made them genuinely useful rather than frustratingly imprecise. A task that used to require half an hour of searching through documentation and a series of attempts can now be approached in a few minutes, with a working draft as a starting point.

This change in workflow frees up time and attention for the activities that language models still do not handle well: genuinely understanding the problem the software must solve, designing an architecture that holds up under pressure, evaluating the trade-offs between different solutions while accounting for the specific context of a project, and communicating effectively with people who have objectives and vocabularies different from the developer’s own. These are deeply human skills, and the fact that the mechanical part of the work is being automated tends to make them more visible and more valuable, not less.

What changes for juniors and seniors

The impact is not uniform across the entire career of a developer. For those just starting out, the availability of highly capable AI tools creates an interesting tension: on one hand it enormously simplifies access to working solutions, allowing even inexperienced developers to produce functioning code in reasonable time, while on the other it risks skipping the laborious learning phase in which one truly understands why a given solution works and another does not. Writing hundreds of trivial functions is tedious, but it is in that process that a sensitivity for code develops, one that language models are not yet able to convey. Training programs will probably need to evolve to address this tension, giving more space to critical analysis and understanding of fundamentals, rather than building competence through the repetition of mechanical exercises.

For experienced professionals, the change is different but equally significant. A senior developer who knows how to use AI tools well does not simply become faster at doing what they did before, but changes the nature of the questions they are able to answer. They can explore more architectural alternatives in the same amount of time, stay current on technologies they have not used directly, and dedicate more energy to critical review and less to mechanical writing. The risk in this case is yielding to the temptation of trusting automatic suggestions without verifying them in depth, losing that capacity for independent judgment which is the hardest skill to replace.

The risk of standardization

One of the less discussed problems in the enthusiasm around AI in software development is the tendency toward standardized solutions. Language models are trained on enormous quantities of existing code and tend to produce solutions that reflect the most common patterns in that corpus. This is an advantage in many everyday situations but becomes a limitation when the problem requires a genuinely different approach from those already seen. The most significant technical innovation often comes from those willing to ignore dominant conventions and explore an approach that the majority of developers would not consider, and this is a capability that current models do not possess reliably.

The other risk linked to standardization concerns the diversity of technology ecosystems. When an AI tool systematically suggests the same libraries, the same frameworks, and the same patterns regardless of the specific context, it tends to consolidate the dominance of already dominant technologies and makes it harder to discover alternatives. This is not a theoretical problem, because the history of software development shows that some of the most important innovations came from those who adopted niche technologies early on that major players ignored.

Documentation as a strategic skill

In a context where AI can generate large quantities of code in a short time, the ability to document clearly and usefully becomes more important, not less. Explaining why a certain architectural decision was made, which alternatives were considered and discarded, which assumptions underlie a certain implementation: this type of context is exactly what allows a team to use effectively the code generated with AI assistance, both to maintain it over time and to adapt it when conditions change. Documentation has always had value, but in a workflow accelerated by automatic generation it tends to become the main channel through which knowledge is preserved and passed on within a project.

The future of the profession

Imagining that artificial intelligence will make the developer role obsolete is a simplification that does not hold up to analysis. The software that organizations must build and maintain is not decreasing; on the contrary, the ability to build it more quickly tends to increase demand for new and more complex digital products. What is changing is the profile of those who build that software. Purely executive skills lose centrality in favor of the ability to define the correct problem, to critically evaluate proposed solutions, to communicate clearly among people with different backgrounds, and to maintain an overall vision of systems that are progressively becoming larger and more interconnected. The profession does not disappear but shifts toward a dimension in which judgment, experience, and the capacity to reason under uncertainty matter more than typing speed.
